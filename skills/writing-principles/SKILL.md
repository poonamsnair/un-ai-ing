# writing-principles

Use this as the writing standard for every un-AI-ing rewrite. `rewrite-draft` writes to it; `rewrite-verify` scores against it.

## Non-negotiables

- Keep the draft's meaning, facts, numbers, legal or technical terms, and uncertainty unless a reviewer mark asks for a change.
- Do not invent claims, evidence, thresholds, obligations, citations, examples, or source support.
- Accuracy beats elegance. Keep a passive construction, long sentence, or technical term when it genuinely improves precision or focus.
- Use style references only for rhythm, sentence shape, specificity, and formality. Do not copy their facts, topic, examples, or distinctive wording.
- Cut clutter, throat-clearing, repeated claims, weak transitions, and inflated language.

# 100 Keys to Good Writing

*Distilled from: The Sense of Style (Pinker), On Writing Well (Zinsser), The Oxford Essential Guide to Writing, Style: Lessons in Clarity and Grace (Williams), and Bird by Bird (Lamott)*

---

## I. CLARITY

**1. Clarity is the essential virtue of words.** If your reader has to re-read a sentence to understand it, you've failed. Everything else — beauty, rhythm, voice — is secondary to being understood.

**2. Clear thinking becomes clear writing.** You cannot write clearly if you haven't thought clearly. A muddy thinker cannot produce good English, no matter how large their vocabulary.

**3. Prose is a window onto the world.** In classic style, writing succeeds when it aligns language with truth. The proof of success is clarity and simplicity.

**4. The writer sees something the reader hasn't noticed.** Good writing orients the reader's gaze so she can see the truth for herself. Your job is presentation.

**5. Don't make the reader work harder than necessary.** Every sentence should be immediately parseable. If a reader gets lost, there's no guarantee they'll come back.

**6. Imagine the reader looking over your shoulder.** Write as though explaining something to an intelligent friend who simply hasn't seen what you've seen yet.

**7. The reader is someone with an attention span of about 30 seconds.** Respect that. Earn their continued attention sentence by sentence.

**8. If you suspect a sentence may puzzle a reader, it will.** Trust your instinct. If you hesitate about clarity, revise immediately.

---

## II. SIMPLICITY & ECONOMY

**9. Clutter is the disease of American writing.** We strangle in unnecessary words, circular constructions, pompous frills, and meaningless jargon.

**10. Omit needless words.** Strunk's Rule 17. Every word should serve a purpose. If it doesn't push the sentence forward, cut it.

**11. Use short words over long ones when they mean the same thing.** "Help" not "assistance." "Many" not "numerous." "Ease" not "facilitate." "First" not "initial." "Do" not "implement."

**12. Beware the long word that's no better than the short word.** "Remainder" (rest), "individual" (man or woman), "sufficient" (enough), "utilize" (use) — these add nothing but syllables.

**13. Strip every sentence to its barest bones.** Then see what's left. If your meaning survives, you had clutter. If it doesn't, add back only what's needed.

**14. Simplify, simplify.** Thoreau's advice applies to writing as much as to life. Each unnecessary word dilutes the ones that matter.

**15. A large part of revision is chipping away unnecessary words.** Be ruthless. Love cutting. Press the delete key and watch unnecessary phrases vanish.

**16. Most adverbs are unnecessary.** If you choose a verb with a specific meaning, don't add an adverb that carries the same meaning. Don't say the radio "blared loudly" — "blare" connotes loudness.

**17. Don't tell the reader something twice.** The inevitable loose ends of every first draft include redundancies. Find them and eliminate them.

**18. Concision is brevity relative to purpose.** It's not about being short for shortness's sake. It's about using no more words than the idea requires.

---

## III. STRONG VERBS & ACTIVE VOICE

**19. Verbs are the most important of all your tools.** They push the sentence forward and give it momentum. Active verbs push hard; passive verbs tug fitfully.

**20. The difference between an active-verb style and a passive-verb style is the difference between life and death.** "Joe saw him" is strong. "He was seen by Joe" is weak. The first is short and precise.

**21. Active verbs enable us to visualize an activity.** They require a subject — a pronoun, a noun, a person — to put them in motion. The reader can see something happening.

**22. Many verbs carry in their imagery or sound a suggestion of what they mean.** Glitter, dazzle, plunge, swoop, swerve, clobber, smash — use verbs that do double duty.

**23. Avoid "zombie nouns" (nominalizations).** Don't embalm a spry verb into a lifeless noun. Instead of "effect an implementation," just "implement." Instead of "make a decision," just "decide."

**24. Bring nominalized nouns back to life as verbs.** "There was a cancellation of the event" becomes "They cancelled the event." Restore the actor and the action.

**25. Don't bury the action in a noun and then prop it up with a weak verb.** "Conducted an investigation" becomes "investigated." "Made an adjustment" becomes "adjusted."

**26. Use the passive voice deliberately, not by default.** The passive has its place — when the actor is unknown, unimportant, or when you want to emphasize the recipient. But make it a choice, not a tic.

---

## IV. VOICE & PERSONALITY

**27. Good writing has personality.** Readers enjoy sensing a mind at work, hearing a clear voice, responding to an unusual sensibility.

**28. Don't be afraid to express your own opinions and feelings.** You are a vital part of the subject. You are really writing about how you understand it, how you feel about it.

**29. We should hear a voice beyond sheer clarity and coherence.** The voice readers hear contributes substantially to the character they construct of the writer.

**30. Classic style is confident about its own voice.** If you're not comfortable using an expression without apologetic quotation marks, you probably shouldn't be using it at all.

**31. Develop a voice that readers will recognize on the page.** A voice that's enjoyable not only in its musical line but in its avoidance of sounds that would cheapen its tone: breeziness, condescension, and clichés.

**32. Write with your whole self.** If genuine feeling and intelligence come through, you will be interesting. Interest lies at the center of good writing.

**33. Your persona must be authentic.** Authenticity means that the personality readers sense in your words is the personality you want them to perceive.

**34. Your persona must also be appropriate.** It should be efficacious — achieving your ends with your specific audience.

**35. Find the best writers in fields that interest you and read their work aloud.** Get their voice and their taste into your ear — their attitude toward language. You will eventually shed those skins and become who you're supposed to become.

---

## V. UNITY & TONE

**36. Unity is the anchor of good writing.** Every piece should have one consistent subject, one prevailing mood, one governing attitude.

**37. Pick a tone and stick with it.** Casual, formal, conversational — any tone is acceptable. But don't mix two or three.

**38. Don't mix casual breeziness with serious formality.** Such fatal mixtures are common in writers who haven't learned control. They unsettle the reader.

**39. Ask yourself: In what capacity am I going to address the reader?** As a teacher? A friend? An expert? A fellow traveler? Decide early and be consistent.

**40. Ask yourself: What pronoun and tense am I going to use?** First person or third? Past tense or present? Choose and commit. Don't waver mid-piece.

---

## VI. COHERENCE & FLOW

**41. Whenever one sentence comes after another, readers need to see a connection.** So eager are readers to seek coherence that they will supply it even when none exists. Don't make them guess.

**42. A coherent text is one in which the reader always knows which relation holds between one sentence and the next.** Is it cause and effect? Elaboration? Contrast? Example? Make it clear.

**43. Coherence begins with the writer and reader being clear about the topic.** Announce it. Don't assume the reader will figure it out from scattered hints.

**44. Good writing takes advantage of a reader's expectations of where to go next.** It accompanies the reader on a journey, arranging material in a logical sequence: general to specific, big to small, early to late.

**45. Keep topic strings consistent.** When your subjects shift erratically from sentence to sentence, readers lose their bearings. Keep a steady focus.

**46. Put old information before new information.** Open sentences with what the reader already knows; close with what's new. This creates a natural forward momentum.

**47. Paragraph unity involves coherence and flow.** Coherence means the ideas fit together. Flow means the sentences link up so readers are not conscious of gaps.

**48. Use transitions as railroad switches.** They smooth and ease the turn from one track to another. But don't over-signpost — a well-structured argument needs fewer transitions than a disorganized one.

**49. Repeat key words to link sentences naturally.** Verbal repetition is the simplest connection between sentences. A repeated word makes a strong, clear link.

**50. Use conjunctive adverbs to show logical relationships.** Therefore, however, consequently, thus, furthermore, moreover, nonetheless — but only when the logic wouldn't be obvious without them.

---

## VII. EMPHASIS & POSITIONING

**51. The two most emphatic positions in a sentence are the opening and the closing.** Put your most important information in one of these positions.

**52. Place key words at the end of the sentence.** The end carries the heaviest stress. Readers naturally lean forward into what comes last.

**53. Trim the end of sentences until you reach the information you want to stress.** Lop off unnecessary trailing words so your key idea lands in the final stressed position.

**54. Open with key words when you want immediate impact.** "Dead — that's what the Big Bull Market was." Immediately, readers see what matters.

**55. Strengthen important points by expressing them in short or unusual sentences.** A short sentence after several long ones carries tremendous punch. It stays in the reader's ear.

**56. Use interrupting modifiers to throw weight on important ideas.** A phrase set off by commas or dashes forces the reader to pause, concentrating attention on what follows.

**57. Writing in which every sentence is emphatic is like having someone shout at you.** Emphasis depends on contrast. Reserve your strongest effects for moments that deserve them.

**58. Avoid ending sentences with metadiscourse.** Nothing ends a sentence more anticlimactically than "in this regard" or "as mentioned above."

---

## VIII. SENTENCE CRAFT

**59. Good sentence style requires both recurrence and variety.** Enough sameness to create a pattern; enough difference to keep the reader alert.

**60. Vary the length of your sentences.** If all your sentences move at the same plodding gait, the writing becomes deadly. Mix long with short.

**61. An occasional short sentence can carry a tremendous punch.** It stays in the reader's ear.

**62. A well-managed long sentence can be just as clear and crisp as several short ones.** Let the reader take a breath at reasonable intervals. Use coordinated and parallel elements.

**63. Vary your sentence openings.** Don't start every sentence with the subject. Begin occasionally with a phrase, a clause, a single word.

**64. Rhythm is inevitable and important in prose.** Good rhythm enters into what a sentence says, enhancing and reinforcing its meaning.

**65. Sentence structure should reinforce sense.** The form of the sentence should mirror its content — a list structure for accumulation, a short punch for finality.

**66. Read your sentences aloud.** You'll hear where the trouble lies. If you stumble reciting a sentence, your readers will stumble reading it.

**67. Write entirely by ear.** Consider the sound and rhythm of every sentence. Words have emotional weight beyond their meaning — "serene" vs. "tranquil," one so soft, the other strangely disturbing.

---

## IX. DICTION & WORD CHOICE

**68. Diction — word choice — is the heart of writing.** Sentences and paragraphs are important. But words are fundamental.

**69. Words must be precise.** Precision means words express exactly what you think, feel, see, or hear. It also means establishing an appropriate relationship with your readers.

**70. Be specific, not general.** "Emotion" is general. "Fear" is more specific. "Terror" is more specific still. Name the particular, not the class.

**71. Prefer concrete words to abstract ones.** Concrete details are easier to process than vague abstractions. They let the reader see, hear, touch.

**72. A generalization gains value from illustrative detail.** Don't just say "the play is fascinating." Say how. Cite a few specific moments.

**73. Avoid jargon outside technical discourse.** Jargon is really pretentiousness — a learned and mysterious language designed to impress the non-expert. It obscures rather than clarifies.

**74. Avoid pretentiousness.** Using big words to no purpose except to show off. Pretentiousness results from overreach — trying to sound more knowledgeable than you are.

**75. Beware of clichés.** If you can't think of anything more original than "white as a sheet," you're better off saying simply "very white."

**76. Watch out for mixed metaphors.** "He put his foot in his mouth and jumped off the deep end." These ask us to perceive simultaneously two things that can't go together.

---

## X. FIGURATIVE LANGUAGE

**77. Figures of speech add great richness when woven into prose.** You don't improve writing by sticking in occasional similes. They must be integrated, not decorative.

**78. A good metaphor clarifies, concretizes, and gives pleasure.** It makes abstract ideas tangible. It lets readers see the unfamiliar in terms of the familiar.

**79. Develop your metaphors consistently.** If you compare something to a boxer, let the details extend naturally — elbow, wrist, fist, stance — rather than switching images mid-stream.

**80. Similes give pleasure and sharpen judgment.** They delight by their novelty and aptness. A good simile makes the reader smile in recognition.

**81. Figures of speech work through context.** The total diction — all your words — controls a figurative word, making it fly in the intended direction.

---

## XI. THE WRITING PROCESS

**82. Writing has three steps: thinking about it, doing it, and doing it again.** And again and again, as often as time allows and patience endures.

**83. Writing is an evolving process, not a product.** Nobody expects you to get it right the first time, or even the second time.

**84. The essence of writing is rewriting.** Just because you write with ease doesn't mean you're writing well. The real craft is in revision.

**85. Drafting is spontaneous and active; revision is thoughtful and critical.** When you draft, keep going. When you revise, become a demanding reader who expects perfection.

**86. Most rewriting consists of reshaping and tightening your first try.** Making sure you've given the reader a narrative flow they can follow with no effort.

**87. Learn to enjoy the tidying process.** Love to rewrite. Love to cut. Love to replace a humdrum word with one that has more precision or surprise.

**88. The more you draft, the better the result.** Most people who publish feel they stopped one draft too soon.

**89. Read your draft as a demanding reader, not as the writer.** When you write, you see from inside. When you revise, you must see from outside — finding the gaps your knowledge fills but the reader's won't.

---

## XII. OVERCOMING FEAR

**90. Fear of writing gets planted at an early age.** Usually at school. It never entirely goes away. But it's an unnecessary fear to carry through life.

**91. Writing is not a special language owned by English teachers.** It's available to everyone. It's thinking on paper. Anyone who thinks clearly can write clearly.

**92. The biggest fear for nonfiction writers is not being able to bring off their assignment.** The cure is patience, persistence, and the understanding that the first draft is always bad.

**93. Forget the competition and go at your own pace.** Your only contest is with yourself. The hares who sprint out of the starting gate are overtaken by tortoises who master the craft.

**94. Your subconscious mind does more writing than you think.** Often you'll fight your way through a verbal thicket all day, and overnight the solution will present itself.

---

## XIII. READER AWARENESS & RESPECT

**95. The key to good style is having a clear conception of the make-believe world in which you're pretending to communicate.** Visualize yourself in conversation with the reader. Put words into the mouth of the little avatar who represents you.

**96. The reader is competent.** In classic style, you trust your reader to recognize the truth when she sees it, as long as she's given an unobstructed view.

**97. Don't talk down to your reader.** Condescension is as deadly as obscurity. Treat readers as equals who simply haven't encountered this particular information yet.

**98. Don't lose the reader — there's no sin so grave.** The reader will not easily be lured back. Every sentence is a contract: I won't waste your time, and in return you'll keep reading.

---

## XIV. HABITS & DISCIPLINE

**99. The starting point for becoming a good writer is to be a good reader.** Writers acquire technique by reverse-engineering good prose. Immerse yourself in excellent writing to develop your ear.

**100. With each rewrite, try to force your personality onto the material.** Show up every day, turn on the switch. Nobody is going to do it for you. The blank page is waiting, and the only way to fill it is to write.
